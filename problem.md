This hackathon concept frames the medical logistics problem set around blood, blood products, and the supplies required to collect, store, test, transport, and transfuse them in support of Marine Corps operations. The proposed capability is a decision support tool-enabled layer that uses Defense Health Agency (DHA) medical logistics data to visualize risk across a hub-and-spoke network and predict when a constrained hub will place distributed forces at operational risk. In a USINDOPACOM distributed maritime operations (DMO) scenario, the tool would help commanders, logisticians, and medical planners identify where blood support is most fragile, which spoke locations will fail first, and what mitigation actions must occur before mission impact becomes acute. The operational scenario is a contested maritime fight in which blood and blood-product support is centered on a regional hub with multiple spoke nodes that support dispersed Marine units, afloat elements, and expeditionary medical sites. The hub is degraded by competing global demands, transportation disruption, refrigeration limitations, or a crisis in another theater that constrains lift, donor support, testing capacity, or available inventory. Because blood is perishable and highly sensitive to storage, timing, and traceability, a single hub constraint can quickly cascade across the spoke network. The problem is not limited to packed red blood cells. It also includes plasma, platelets, walking blood bank enablers, blood collection kits, testing supplies, cold-chain equipment, transfusion sets, labels, storage containers, and generators or fuel required to preserve viability at the point of need. A decision support tool should be referenced in the hackathon narrative as the model for fusing operational and logistics data into a common decision picture. A decision support tool prototype could integrate DHA inventory data, expiration windows, route status, refrigeration health, unit demand signals, casualty assumptions, and node-level constraints to show days of supply remaining, projected stock-out dates, route risk, and confidence levels. It should also highlight secondary effects such as the loss of testing consumables, temperature excursions, or transport delays that make blood unavailable even when nominal inventory appears sufficient. This helps demonstrate that the real readiness problem is not simply stock on hand, but whether viable blood support can move from hub to spoke in time to sustain distributed maritime operations. A key feature should be market-aware contingency sourcing. When the hub becomes constrained, the tool should evaluate buy-on-market options for approved commercial acquisition or host-nation sourcing of blood support supplies where lawful, feasible, and operationally appropriate. This would not imply uncontrolled local purchase of blood itself in every case; rather, it would show where market options may relieve pressure on the network by providing approved consumables, refrigeration support, dry ice, validated shipping materials, donor-screening supplies, laboratory reagents, transfusion accessories, or transportation services. The hackathon value is that the prototype would show how DHA data, fused with operational data, can move Marine Corps blood support from reactive resupply to predictive sustainment, giving decision-makers a practical way to preserve survivability when a central hub is constrained by conflict, distance, and competing global demand. Ideal data inputs: blood and component inventory; expiration dates; donor and collection capacity; testing supplies; cold-chain status; route and lift availability; casualty and demand scenarios; substitute sourcing options; and approved buy-on-market vendors for blood-support supplies.
